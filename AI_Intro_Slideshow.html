1 · Foundations

What Is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the field of building computer systems that can perform tasks which normally require human intelligence — like recognizing speech, making decisions, or solving problems.

In short: AI systems take in information, and use it to reason, learn, or decide — instead of just following one fixed, pre-written instruction every time.

You Meet AI Every Day
  • A phone assistant that understands your question
  • A streaming app that predicts what you’ll like
  • A spam filter that learns new junk-mail patterns
  • A photo app that recognizes faces in pictures
1 · Foundations

What Makes a System “Intelligent”?

Researchers generally agree an “intelligent” system shows most of these four abilities:

Perceive

Takes in information from the world — text, images, sound, or sensor data.

Reason

Processes that information to draw conclusions or make a decision.

Learn

Improves its performance over time as it sees more data or feedback.

Adapt

Adjusts its behavior when the situation or input changes.

1 · Foundations

A Closer Look: Is It Really “Intelligent”?

Not all “AI” is the same kind of smart. Most systems today fall into one category:

Narrow AI (what we have today)

  • Extremely good at one specific task — recognizing photos, translating text, recommending songs.
  • Has no understanding beyond that task. A chess-playing AI can’t hold a conversation.
  • This describes every real AI system in use today, including the ones in this class.

General Intelligence (hypothetical)

  • Would reason across any subject the way a human can — learning one skill and applying it somewhere totally new.
  • Would understand context, meaning, and consequences, not just patterns in data.
  • Doesn’t exist yet — it’s a research goal and a topic of ongoing debate, not a product.
Discussion angle: if a system is only ever good at one narrow task, should we still call it “intelligent”? Which of the four traits (perceive, reason, learn, adapt) would a system need to gain before you’d call it truly general?
2 · AI vs Automation

AI vs. Automation: What’s the Difference?

Automation

Follows a fixed set of rules, in the same order, every time. It doesn’t learn or adjust — the same input always gives the same output.

Artificial Intelligence

Uses data to reason, learn, and decide. It can handle new, unexpected situations and often improves the more it’s used.

2 · AI vs Automation

Why the Line Matters in the Real World

This isn’t just a vocabulary exercise — the distinction has real consequences.

Automation is predictable

  • Easy to test — the same input always gives the same output.
  • When it fails, it usually fails obviously (an error message, a jam, a stall).
  • But it can’t handle a situation its rules didn’t anticipate.

AI can be confidently wrong

  • Adapts to new situations — but its reasoning is often a “black box,” hard to fully explain.
  • A mistake may not look like an error at all — a wrong prediction can be delivered as if it were certain.
  • That makes human review and oversight essential, especially for high-stakes decisions.
Why it matters: knowing whether a system is rule-based or AI-driven changes how much you should trust it, how you’d catch its mistakes, and who’s responsible when something goes wrong.

4 · How AI Works

How AI Works: The Basic Pipeline

At a basic level, every AI system follows the same simple flow:

STEP 1

Data

Raw examples the system learns from (photos, text, numbers).

STEP 2

Algorithm

A set of mathematical steps that finds patterns in that data.

STEP 3

Model

The trained “brain” that results — stores the patterns it found.

STEP 4

Prediction

The model applies patterns to new input and gives an output.

4 · How AI Works

A Concrete Example: Recognizing Cats in Photos

Data

Thousands of photos labeled “cat” or “not a cat.”

Algorithm

A learning procedure looks for visual patterns — shapes, edges, textures — that separate the two labels.

Model

The finished, trained network that has “learned” what cat features look like.

Prediction

Shown a brand-new photo, the model outputs “94% likely a cat.”

Key idea: the model never sees a hard-coded rule like “if whiskers, then cat.” It infers its own patterns directly from labeled data.
4 · How AI Works

Garbage In, Garbage Out: Why Data Quality Matters

A model only learns what its training data shows it — gaps and bias in the data become gaps and bias in the model.

Facial Recognition

Early systems were trained mostly on lighter-skinned faces. As a result, some performed noticeably worse at recognizing people with darker skin — not because of the algorithm alone, but because the training data wasn’t representative.

Hiring Algorithms

A tool trained on a company’s past resumes can learn to favor whatever pattern was common in previous hires. If those past hires skewed toward one group, the model can repeat that same bias — automatically, and at scale.

Key idea: a model is only as good as the data it learns from. Incomplete or biased data doesn’t just limit accuracy — it can actively cause unfair or harmful outcomes.
5 · Types of AI

Five Types of AI You’ll Hear About

Machine Learning

Systems that improve at a task by learning from data instead of fixed rules.

Deep Learning

Machine learning using layered “neural networks” loosely inspired by the brain.

Computer Vision

AI that interprets images and video — recognizing objects, faces, or scenes.

Natural Language Processing

AI that understands and generates human language, spoken or written.

Generative AI

AI that creates brand-new text, images, audio, or code from a prompt.
